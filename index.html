NPWT for Small Scale Wounds

A wearable negative pressure wound therapy (NPWT) device.


Project Overview

NPWT is a therapeutic technique using a suction pump, tubing, and a dressing to promote healing in acute or chronic wounds. Some examples of use cases include diabetic wounds, surgical incisions, and second- and third-degree burns. Experts have long agreed that the size of NPWT units make it impractical to treat small wounds, and conventional technologies are limited to clinical settings. The goal of this project was to implement a NPWT at a scale equivalent ot modern wearable technologies.

System Design

The design incoroporates a piezoelectric micropump that is able to generate 100 mbar (75 mmHg) of suction pressure. The device pictured above is the mp6-gas micropump from Bartels Microtechnik GmbH. In order to operate the micropump a piezoeelctric actuating signal is produced by the drive circuit. The drive circuit integrates a battery, the mp6 controller, and auxiliary surface-mounted components.

Results

To first demonstrate the technical feasibility of the design the team sourced the components for the suction mechanism and conducted preliminary trials on a breadboard. Suction pressure, power consumption, and functionality of the pump was noted. After the successful proof-of-concept trial, the team began to design and build a low-fidelity prototype, which is pictured above. The “patch pump” design is inspired by Constant Glucose Monitoring sensors. Delays relating to the COVID-19 pandemic limited the team's ability to progress towards a high-fidelity prototype.

Ping Pong Launcher

A ping pong ball shooter that can hit a bucket in the 5-20 ft. range.

Project Overview

The purpose of this project was to build an experimental prototype of a ping pong ball launcher and to use Design of Experiments (DOE) to model the mechanism according to control factors. The product development process included front-end activities such as product decomposition and FMEA. Developing the predictive model allowed the team to characterize the response of the system, which in this case is how far the ball launches. As a result we are able to adjust these control factors to achieve a desired launch distance.

Working Principle

The dual-flywheel launching mechanism is powered by two DC motors which are speed-controlled via pulse width modulation (PWM). An Arduino Uno is used to read an analog input from the potentiometer and writes the motor signal accordingly. The duty cycle (0-100%) of the motor is also printed on the LCD screen as the potentiometer is turned, although it is difficult to see the text in the illustrations above.

Experimental Analysis

A full-factorial experiment was conducted and the results were analyzed in Minitab. The figures above are sample outputs from this analysis. Response optimization was then performed in Excel to be able to predict how far the ball will launch for a given set of control factors.

Bball Track

A biomechanical study of the free throw using data extracted from OpenPose.


Project Overview

My team was made up of people who liked basketball, and so for our project we decided to study biomechanics of the free throw. The goal of the project was to perform a data-based analysis of the free throw to determine which variables contributed to a successful free throw. By leveraging deep learning the team was able to gain some valuable insight on this.

Approach

We decided to compare the shooting form of three of the best NBA free throw shooters: Stephen Curry (92%), Damian Lillard (93%), and Karl-Anthony Towns (88%) to three novice shooters who averaged ~ 40%: Alex, Chris (me), and Thomas. Based on our literature review the team had a good hypothesis of which parameters would be most important. Using OpenPose (github here), which is an open-source key point detecion system dveloepd by researchers at Carnegie Mellon University, the team was able to extract frame-by-frame position coordinates for segments of interest. The raw OpenPose data was then post-processed to yield angles and angular velocities that could be compared between the skill groups.

Analysis

The conclusions from the study are summarized below.

  1. Trunk Inclination and Centre of Mass

    The data showed no significant difference in trunk inclination at release, with both professional and novice shooters being in an upright position. However, it was clear that the novice shooters had higher COM velocities than the pros. This suggests that less COM motion during the free throw is better.

  2. Upper Arm Angular Displacements and Velocities

    The bubble graphs shown below are a good way to visualize the difference between the two skill groups. Professionals exhibit greater shoulder flexion at release, which leads to greater release height and thus a greater margin for error in launch angle and speed. Since the free throw does not include a jump, the release velocity is largely determined by upper arm angular velocities. The elbow angular velocities of the pros are all signifcantly higher than the shoulder angular velocities at release.

  3. Knee Flexion and Extension

    There are two phases of the free throw as it relates to the knee and these can be visualized from the single shot profile (Steph Curry) in the figure below. The first is a flexion phase (downward slope) as the shooter crouches, and the second is an extension phase leading up to release (upward slope). From a biomechanical standpoint, greater flexion results in a more forceful extension, and so some experts have advocated for a deep crouch prior to the free throw. The data says otherwise. NBA players showed less knee flexion prior to release (higher min. knee angle - horizontal axis) than the novice group. A reason for this is that ankle flexion is also used for lower-body force production.

Architecture Detection

A deep convolutional neural network classifier for achitectural styles implemented using PyTorch.


Project Overview

In this project, my team and I used transfer learning to solve an image classification problem of our choice. We chose to classify architectural styles because of their cultural importance as well as the granular differences between them. The dataset used for the project can be found here. Because of class overlap we decided to classify the styles according to time period instead of the initial 25 classes. The data thus had to be regrouped before it was cleaned, split into train-validation-test format, and augmented.

Model Architecture and Training

The model architecture is depicted in the image above. It consists of a ResNet-50 convolutional base followed by dropout and fully-connected layers for classification. The ResNet base is intialized with pre-trained ImageNet weights and the last two blocks are fine-tuned at a low learning rate, whereas the classification layers are trained at a higher learning rate (10x). This training approach was found to work best due to the size of the dataset and also the dissimilarity between the architecural style dataset and ImageNet.

Quantitative Results

The models loss and accuracy were tracked during training. The learning rates were decayed as training progressed to allow for better convergence. Overall, the traning curve suggests that the model is overfit to the training data in spite of techniques such as drop out, weight decay, and reducing network capacity. Class overlap is believed to be a major barrier in achieving a generalization accuracy greater than 90%. The final accuracy on the holdout dataset was 81.5%.

Confusion matrices were used to gauge class-to-class performance. The one shown above is for the holdout dataset. The model is able to identify architecture belonigng to Mediterranean and Middle Eastern Civilations with ease, but it struggles with Neoclassicism, often confusing it with Renaissance. This is not surpising as these two styles are closely related.

Additional testing was performed on >1000 web-scraped images using the Flickr API. The model achieved a classification accuracy of 65% on this data.

Hobbyist 3D Printer

A mechanical design project for use in a home or small office setting.

In this conceptual design project my team and I authored engineering specifications, generated and evaluated proposed concepts according to objective criteria, and developed an accurate CAD model of the chosen design. An exhaustive BOM was also generated for the device. The images shown above are rendered images created using SolidWorks.

Design

The final design is a Cartesian XY-Hotend Z-Bed printer, also known as a CoreXY printer. These printers are able to achieve fast print speeds and large build areas. The underlying mechanism is a two-belt pulley system driven by two stepper motors positioned at corners horizontal from one another. Timing belts are used to control the XY movement of the extruder with high resolution and minimal backlash. Drawings were created for key mechanical assemblies. On the left is a top view of the entire printer and on the right is an orthographic view of the y axis carriage.
